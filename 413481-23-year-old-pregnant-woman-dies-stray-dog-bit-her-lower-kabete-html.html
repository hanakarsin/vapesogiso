An elderly woman working as a househelp for one of Mike Sonko's aides has lost her daughter to what they termed as medical negligence after she suffered a dog bite.

Concepta Nafula's 23-year-old daughter was seven months pregnant when the unfortunate encounter with a stray dog happened on Saturday, May 22, in Lower Kabete.

Sharing the sad news on his Facebook page, Ben Mulwa said the mom-to-be and her unborn son succumbed on Monday, May 24, due to gross mismanagement of the situation.

Dog bites

Well, dogs may be our best friends, but dogs are still animals and they can bite.

If a dog bite pierces a person’s skin, bacteria from the animal’s mouth can get into the body, which can cause an infection.

According to medical news, people with diabetes, liver disease, illnesses that suppress the immune system, and other health conditions may be at greater risk for a more severe infection.

Washing the wound thoroughly can remove the bacteria from the body and help prevent infection.

If the bacteria stay in the body, they can cause an infection, such as tetanus, rabies, or sepsis.

Washing a dog bite wound with soap and running water is vital in the treatment of rabies, a viral zoonotic disease that accounts for 2000 deaths in Kenya annually.

Stray dogs in Nairobi

The animal welfare group in 2019 opposed Nairobi City County's intentions to kill over 50,000 stray dogs in the city, suggesting the cash set aside should be spent on vaccination and sterilisation.

World Animal Protection, based in London, claimed that killing dogs would be inhumane and that the KSh 100 million City Hall had set aside for dog culling could be better spent protecting them.

“We urge the county government to immediately abandon plans to kill stray and roaming dogs," said Tennyson Williams, the World Animal Protection director. "The authorities can find better means to manage the increasing dog population in Nairobi and its environs,” he added.

According to the group, the KSh 100 million set aside to cull dogs could be used to vaccinate all canines in Nairobi for seven years at a cost of KSh 300 every vaccine cycle.

The lobby further argued culling dogs was a cruel, needless and ineffective attempt to control rabies and manage stray dog populations.
